Pfizer and the COVID-19 Vaccine¶

Pfizer Inc. (\$PFE) is a biopharmaceutical company headquartered in New York City. The company IPO'ed on June 1st, 1972, and as of market close on October 9th, 2020 its shares are trading at $36.79.

Pfizer is widely considered to be a "blue chip" stock. The company has existed and has produced vaccines and drug delivery technology since 1882, when it developed the smallpox vaccine. Current annual revenue and market capitalization put the company squarely in the "large-cap" category of stocks, and Pfizer boasts a dividend yield of 4.12 percent, making it popular among conservatives investors.

As one of the pharmaceutical companies working on a COVID-19 vaccine, Pfizer is positioned to receive a massive windfall as a producer and distributor of the highly anticipated drug. Virtually all who can get the vaccine will want it (save for those who are anti-vax), both in the US and globally.

In May of 2020, Pfizer claimed that a vaccine would be ready "in the October time-frame" and that it would be prepared to distribute hundreds of millions of doses of the vaccine in early 2021. So far that estimate is incorrect, though there was (and still is) a major push to have a vaccine by Election Day. In July, it was revealed that the US government had signed an agreement with Pfizer for the delivery of 100 million doses of the vaccine, with the ability to acquire another 500 million doses. (https://www.hhs.gov/about/news/2020/07/22/us-government-engages-pfizer-produce-millions-doses-covid-19-vaccine.html)

Yet the production of this vaccine exposes Pfizer to some risks. Foremost, no vaccine has ever been developed, approved, and released in such a short timeframe. While the FDA has stringent requirements for new drug approval, there is political pressure from the Trump administration and from the pharma industry to fast-track the vaccine as it represents the best way to help the world return to some modicum of economic and social normalcy. The US government has coined the collaborative effort to create a vaccine as quickly as possible as "Operation Warp Speed." (https://www.politico.com/news/2020/10/09/coronavirus-vaccine-pfizer-election-day-428371)

If, somehow, the Pfizer vaccine is found to be faulty (perhaps due to a lack of long-term human drug trials) the company's reputation will take a major hit, significant amounts of R&D will have been wasted, already produced doses will have to be rejected, and more time will be needed to develop and approve a better version. This is probably the worst case scenario, and would not only negatively impact Pfizer's share price, but the entire global market and social order.

A more likely negative outcome is that the distribution of the vaccine is delayed beyond the targets set earlier in 2020. Stocks would probably slump (as they have numerous times already from bad news regarding a vaccine) but the effect on Pfizer's share price could be minimal as investors continue to anticipate a major jump when a viable vaccine is released.

Takeaways¶

While the pandemic has had obvious effects on working Americans as unemployment rose to 15 percent and then "recovered" to 8 percent (where under 4 percent has been the norm), the effect on the stock market is more difficult to summarize.

Though there were major "red" days or even months, the overall market has all but recovered as it appears that investors who trust that the pandemic will eventually pass attempt to "buy the dip." Emotions are high as positive news concerning vaccine progress or fiscal stimulus is met with major surges in the market, while negative news can cause multiple day dips.

The market reaction to COVID-19 can be defined, foremost, by volatility.

Takeaways¶

Upon closer examination it would appear that, while vaccine breakthroughs tend to occur before a run-up in share price, there can often be a slight dip first. Further, the releases in the late '80s and '90s seems to have insulated Pfizer from the dot-com crash, as there was a steady increase (with minimal volatility) from 1995 to 2000.

We want to avoid going too far into technical analysis of the chart movements particularly as we are not, at this point, considering the company's balance sheet during these periods - only share price and vaccine breakthroughs.

Pfizer has many drug licenses and other revenue producing products outside of their vaccines, as well as numerous subsidiaries that develop a large range of biopharmaceutical products -- vaccines do not tell the whole story.

That being said, a COVID-19 vaccine would be much more highly publicized and more widely distributed than any of the nine vaccines on our list. There are guaranteed orders in the hundreds of millions of doses already lined up. It is not a stretch to say that any pharmaceutical company that gets a vaccine approved will have a jump in share price. The important question, both for Pfizer and the world, is "when will the vaccine be ready?"

Options Strategies for Pfizer¶

Overall, this analysis is bullish on Pfizer -- they are among the biggest companies in the world, they are attractive to conservatives investors due to the high dividend, and they will most likely, eventually, develop a vaccine for COVID-19.

This analysis also recognizes the extreme levels of volatility in this market. Emotions have dictated market movements as people everywhere attempt to deal with economic and social fallout of the pandemic. The upcoming Presidential election is the most contentious in American history. The market was overdue for a correction or even recession according to some top economists before COVID-19 was the dominating news topic.

We will look into two options strategies for investing in Pfizer: a shorter-term neutral strategy that attempts to exploit the volatility as a result of the election and continued vaccine uncertainty, and a longer-term bullish strategy based on the promise of a vaccine and the windfall it will bring.

Iron Condor for Short-Term Neutral Strategy¶

According to Investopedia, an iron condor is typically a neutral strategy and profits the most when the underlying asset doesn't move much. Both profit and risk are capped.

The iron condor is composed of four options: a bought put further OTM, a sold put closer to the money, a bought call further OTM, and a sold call closer to the money.

Ideally we want all the options to expire worthless.

As mentioned, Pfizer is currently trading at $36.79. As we want this to be a short-term strategy (taking advantage of the upcoming election) and minimize the risk of a large movement (such as if Pfizer announces success with the vaccine) we will look at calls and puts for November 6th, 2020.

A long put with a strike price of \$31 has an ask price of \\$0.09 (to be bought).

A short put with a strike price of \$35 has a bid price of \\$0.71 (to be sold).

A long call with a strike price of \$43 has an ask price of \\$0.21 (to be bought).

A short call with a strike price of \$39 has a bid price of \\$0.55 (to be sold).

So, we are selling a put and a call, and receiving \$71 + \\$55 = \$126.

And buying a put and a call for \$9 + \\$21 = \$30.

Once the orders are filled we have a \$96 profit -- our max profit.

Our max loss is the strike price of the long call - the strike price of the short call - net premium received (we are not considering commission -- let's pretend we are using a commission-free trading platform):

\$43 - \\$39 - \$96 = \\$-92 -- our max loss.

Let's take a look at the profit potential as Pfizer's stock price moves.

Call Backspread for Longer-Term Bullish Strategy¶

We also want to setup a trade that is bullish on Pfizer in the longer term - say 6-8 months where there is a higher likelihood that the vaccine is distributed, enough people are vaccinated to achieve herd immunity, and economic recovery can begin.

However, we do want to limit the downside potential in the unlikely case that the vaccine is released but has disastrous consequences, such as unforseen side-effects. Social upheaval after the Presidential election is also a limited risk, but is another potential downside that should be hedged against.

We will explore using a call backspread. This strategy is best used when there is significant upside market movement predicted. The risk is limited and the payoff is unlimited. The downside is that maximum loss occurs when the share price increases a bit, but not a lot.

We will look at calls for June 18th, 2021.

A call backspread consists of selling one ITM call and buying two OTM calls, with one higher than the other.

A short call with a strike price of \$35 has a bid price of \\$3.80

Two long calls with a strike price of \$40 have an ask price of \\$1.93 each.

So we are selling one call and receiving \$380.

We are buying two calls for \$193 = \\$386

Once the orders are filled we have a loss of \$6.

If Pfizer's strike price goes below \$35, our two long calls expire worthless and the loss remains at \\$6

Our max loss is the strike price of the long call - the strike price of the short call x 100 - net premium.

(\$40 - \\$35) x 100 - (-\$6) = \\$506

Max profit is unlimited once the share price surpasses \$45

Conclusions¶

In this project we have seen:

  1. COVID-19 has created irrational, volatile market conditions.

  2. Pfizer's major drug releases can help to insulate the company against unfavorable market conditions, and the stock price usually rises soon after major releases.

A COVID-19 vaccine would be the most important product released by Pfizer since their smallpox and polio drugs, which were developed before the company went public.

By utilizing an iron condor in the short-term, an investor can position themselves to take advantage of market volatility and uncertainty leading up to the release of a vaccine and American Presidential election.

By utilizing a call backspread with a longer 6-8 month term, an investor can hedge against any major drop in share price (in the case of a catastrophic vaccine release) while allowing for unlimited upside potential if the vaccine is a resounding success. This strategy would lose the most if the share price has only a small increase.
